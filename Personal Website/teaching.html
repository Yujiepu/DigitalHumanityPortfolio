I weave narratives that bridge sweeping socio-political transformations with individual experiences. In my Premodern Chinese History course, students explore stories such as a Chinese sailor in 1881 who sent cookies from Britain on the eve of the Sino-Japanese War, revealing how global tensions shaped personal lives. In Mental Health in East Asia, Past and Present, we examine societal pressures and personal tragedies through The Memoirs of Lady Hyegyeong, which chronicles the Crown Prince Sado incident in Choson Korea. These case studies show how micro-histories illuminate broader historical forces.

By centering intimate accounts alongside grand epochs, I encourage students to scrutinize primary sources, question established narratives, and appreciate the human dimensions of past events. Narrative-driven discussions spark curiosity and deepen analytical skills as learners contextualize individual stories within larger political and cultural frameworks. Ultimately, my teaching philosophy holds that history comes alive when we navigate both its grand structures and personal stories, cultivating empathy and a nuanced grasp of the past.
