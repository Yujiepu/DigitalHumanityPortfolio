Rather than focusing on asylums, it examines the judiciary, an integral arm of the bureaucracy, as the primary arena where notions of mental disorder were negotiated and enforced. Supported by fellowships from the Graduate College and the Center for East Asian and Pacific Studies, I spent a year in mainland Chinese archives studying administrative handbooks, memorials to the throne, homicide case files in the Routine Memorials collection and Ba-County archives, alongside Manchu medical texts. I show how commoners, local physicians, magistrates, and Ministry of Justice officials engaged in a bottom-up dialogue over terminology, diagnostic criteria, treatment efficacy, and legal responsibility, ultimately enshrining medical assessments as authoritative in insanity trials.

This project challenges the prevailing view that modern psychiatric concepts arrived in China only through Western imports. Instead, it uncovers an indigenous evolution of medical authority, socially embraced and legally sanctioned within Qing legal practice that deliberately marginalized religious explanations of madness. By tracing how medical expertise was woven into bureaucratic processes before Western psychiatry‚Äôs influence, it calls for a reassessment of Eurocentric assumptions about the origins of modern medicine and psychiatry and highlights a uniquely Chinese pathway to understanding and treating mental disturbance.
